I read about Nashville’s Declaration of Transportation Independence and it made my heart swell with the fond recollection of how cheap, safe, public transport gave me my independence.

In 2003, I moved from Doha, Qatar to Chennai, India. Up until then my friends and I hung out at shopping malls. Saving up money from doing my chores and getting my face painted in the mall, or buying a happy meal, was all that I was allowed to do by myself. Everything else required parental supervision. It was impossible for me to break that rule because even if I got out of the house, I had no way of getting to wherever I need to go.

In Chennai, there was the MTC bus. It was cheap enough that I could pay for it with my own money. However, it was also unsafe, in the eyes of my mother, and I was not allowed to take it. Most of my friends took the bus, it was only me and other NRI (Non-resident Indian) kids that didn't. The reasons for the bias against the bus were lost on me at that time. I was genuinely mystified and slightly terrified to try the bus, so it didn’t seem odd that my mother disapproved. However, my friends who had grown up in the city had no such issues.

By the time I was 14, having to plead with my mum to drive me to the beach, or a friend’s place, was suffocating. I couldn't hang out with my friends after school because I would miss the auto-rickshaw meant to take me home. However, I missed the auto one day, and headed to the pay phone to call my mum to come pick me up. I met a neighbor of mine one the way who was heading to take the bus. I had the Rs. 5 fair required to take me home on the 5E bus and so decided to let this girl (who was a couple of years younger than me and much more street smart) guide me home. I remember the joy I felt getting on the bus and paying the fare. Perhaps, it was the teenage thrill of breaking a rule, but my experience with public transit since has lead me to believe that it was the first time I felt independent.

My mother was livid and we fought about me using the bus. During that time, I once paid for the bus with a combination of 25 and 5 Paise coins. The bus conductor was both very annoyed and mildly amused, and after yelling at me for a couple of minutes, gave me my ticket. My mum eventually relented and my own fears gave way to a deep love for cheap public transportation.

Chennai now has a shiny, expensive, metro that is only convenient to get me from the airport to my home but it's the MTC bus that moves me and my heart.
